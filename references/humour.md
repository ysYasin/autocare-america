# Humour — AutoCare America

## The rule

Humour is incidental, never performed. We do not set up jokes. We do not have a "brand personality" that requires wit. When something is funny, it is because the underlying fact is absurd — and we let the fact do the work.

Readers can tell when humour is forced. If the observation doesn't come naturally while writing the sentence, cut it and move on. Authentic dry delivery lands. Performed wit doesn't.

---

## Hook: the first 50 words

Every article must have one dry or absurd observation within the first 50 words. This is the only place in the article where a humorous touch is expected rather than incidental. Its job is to signal that this isn't a generic guide — that there is a person behind it who finds the subject slightly absurd in the right ways.

The observation must be grounded in a real mechanical fact. The absurdity comes from the fact itself, not from trying to be funny about it.

**Good hook (dry pivot):**

> Jiffy Lube performs roughly three oil changes a minute across its 2,000 US locations. That's impressive throughput — and the exact reason you should not bring a transmission problem there.

The dry pivot is the move: state the impressive/absurd fact, then use "and that's why" or "which is exactly why" to deliver the real point. The reader is slightly amused and immediately curious about what comes next.

**What makes a good first-50-words observation:**
- An unexpectedly specific number that reframes the subject ("a franchise chain has done a billion oil changes" → "and yet")
- A counterintuitive consequence of something true ("the thing that makes them fast is the thing that makes them unreliable for X")
- A flat statement of something absurd that most articles would bury or omit

**What does not work as a hook:**
- Forced self-awareness ("Now, before you think this is a hit piece on Jiffy Lube...")
- Manufactured tension ("What your oil change place doesn't want you to know")
- Any observation that could appear in a generic competitor's article

## What works

**Dry observation stated flatly:**

> The 10mm socket is the single most-used size in automotive repair — and the most frequently lost. Buy a set that includes two 10mm sockets or buy a second one separately.

The joke is in the parenthetical truth. No winking. No "ha!" The reader gets it or they don't.

**Honest self-deprecation about the limits of our advice:**

> A belt routing diagram is stamped under your hood on most vehicles, but it's often faded or covered in grease. Your photo is insurance.

Acknowledging that the ideal world and the real world differ — without making it a comedy bit.

**Understated consequence:**

> Do not drain immediately after highway driving: oil at full operating temperature can reach 220°F and will burn you badly.

The deadpan delivery makes the warning land harder than a dramatic version would.

---

## What does not work

- Puns about cars ("revving up your knowledge," "shifting gears," "driving home the point") — do not use these
- Exclamation marks used as a substitute for wit
- Self-conscious asides ("but seriously though")
- Pop culture references — they date quickly and mean nothing to half the audience
- Anything that requires the reader to know the joke to follow the repair instruction
- Charged or controversial observations — the goal is to connect, not to alienate a segment of readers over something unrelated to cars
- Hyper-specific cultural references that only land for one demographic slice of the audience

---

## Frequency

One dry observation per article is plenty. Two is acceptable if both land naturally. Anything more reads like we are trying.

Sparing use is the point. A well-placed observation in an otherwise straight technical article stands out because the rest of the piece is straight. It creates a moment the reader remembers. Heavy-handed humour flattens that effect immediately.

---

## Missing the mark

Not every attempt lands, and that is fine. Even well-calibrated dry observations occasionally fall flat for a particular reader. The failure mode to avoid is not "a joke that doesn't work" — it is "a joke that makes the reader question whether we know what we are talking about." Keep the observation grounded in a real mechanical fact and the worst outcome is that a reader shrugs and keeps reading.

---

## The 10mm rule

If a humorous aside requires explanation, cut it. Humour that needs a footnote is not humour — it is a footnote.
