# Voice — AutoCare America

## Who we are writing for

A car owner who is competent, not necessarily mechanical. They Googled the problem before calling a shop. They want to know if this is a $30 fix or a $1,200 fix before they commit to either. They are skeptical of both mechanics who upsell and internet guides that oversimplify.

---

## Sentence rhythm

Short declarative sentences. One idea per sentence. Vary length — a short sentence after two medium ones has impact. Never start with "It is important to note that." Just say the thing.

**Good:** An oil change takes 30 minutes and costs $25 in materials.

**Bad:** It is important to understand that an oil change, when performed correctly, can be completed in approximately 30 minutes and the materials needed typically cost around $25.

---

## How to open every piece

**Two jobs, first 50 words:** the hook earns the reader's attention; the answer earns their trust. You need both. The hook comes first.

The hook is one dry observation or absurd true fact that signals a real person wrote this. See `humour.md` → "Hook: the first 50 words" for mechanics and examples.

After the hook, give the direct answer — not context, not history, not stakes. The answer.

**Good (hook → answer):**

> Jiffy Lube performs roughly three oil changes a minute across its 2,000 US locations. That's impressive throughput — and the exact reason you should not bring a transmission problem there.
>
> The seven major car service franchise chains handle tens of millions of service visits a year. A few are genuinely good. A few will sell you the wrong repair at full price. Here's how to tell which is which.

**Bad (no hook, generic open):**

> Car service franchise chains handle oil changes, brakes, tires, and transmission work. The major players have thousands of locations across the US. For routine maintenance, they are a reasonable option.

The bad version could have been written by anyone about any topic with minor edits. The good version couldn't.

## Vocabulary

**Use:**
- Specific part names (serpentine belt, caliper piston, drain petcock, mass airflow sensor)
- Specific measurements (ft-lbs, mm, cSt, g/s, RPM, °F)
- Dollar amounts when relevant — always specific, never "a few dollars"
- Trade names where they help (Dexron, Mercon, ATF+4, EPDM, OBD-II)

**Avoid:**
- "seamless," "leverage," "unlock," "world-class," "game-changer"
- "in today's fast-paced world"
- "it's worth noting that"
- "at the end of the day"
- "cutting-edge," "state-of-the-art"
- "comprehensive," "robust," "holistic"
- "peace of mind"
- Passive constructions where active is possible

---

## Tone

Knowledgeable without condescension. We assume the reader is an adult. We do not explain what a wrench is. We do explain that the drain plug comes out counterclockwise and that oil at operating temperature can reach 220°F and will burn you badly — because those are the details that matter.

Honest without being alarmist. "This can damage the catalytic converter if you keep driving" is accurate. "This will destroy your engine immediately" usually is not.

Never cheerful. Never use an exclamation mark. The goal is to sound like the most knowledgeable person in the room who is also the least likely to waste your time.

---

## When to tell people not to hire us / not to DIY

This is the biggest voice tell. Every article should have at least one specific scenario where we say the job is not worth attempting without professional equipment or a specific skill set.

Not: "Always consult a professional mechanic for safety-critical repairs." (boilerplate — delete it)

Instead: "Stop and call a shop if you find a seized caliper that won't compress or retract, or brake lines that feel soft or look corroded. These are safety-critical issues where guessing costs lives, not just money."

---

## Numbers

Always specific. Never round.

- Bad: "Most engines need oil changes every few thousand miles"
- Good: "Modern engines with full-synthetic oil go 7,500 to 10,000 miles between changes"

- Bad: "Battery load tests cost a small fee"
- Good: "Most AutoZone, O'Reilly, and Advance Auto Parts stores do this free with a digital conductance tester"

---

## Format rules

- **Bold** the key term or finding in a list item, then explain it
- Use `<h2>` for main sections, `<h3>` for sub-sections
- Lists for steps, tools, causes, and comparisons
- Paragraphs for explanation and reasoning
- No bullet points for content that flows naturally as prose
- No more than 4 items in a list before asking whether a table would serve better

---

## Tells that it's AI-written

Delete anything that:
- Starts with "In conclusion," "To summarize," "In this article we will..."
- Ends with "By following these steps, you can..." or "We hope this guide has helped you..."
- Uses "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally" to connect thoughts
- Calls anything "crucial," "vital," "essential" without specifying why
- Says "regular maintenance" without specifying intervals
- Asks a rhetorical question ("Ever wondered why your car makes that noise?")
- Uses three synonyms in a row ("important, critical, and essential")
- Hedges with "may," "might," and "could" when a direct statement is possible
- Refers to "many car owners" or "most drivers" without a data point
